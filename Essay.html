01 — Social Media Algorithms & Echo Chambers +
Kurovski, M. (Host). (2024, October 1). #24: Video recommendations at Facebook with Amey Dharwadker [Audio podcast episode]. In Recsperts — Recommender Systems Experts.
This podcast episode is highly relevant to the topic, offering a firsthand account of how Facebook builds and manages its recommendation algorithms. Dharwadker explains how algorithms continuously reinforce user engagement, creating echo chambers that narrow what people see over time without them realizing it. He also raises concerns about whether optimizing for engagement benefits users or fuels online division, connecting directly to how internet controversy is shaped by platform design. In terms of credibility, although it is a podcast rather than a peer-reviewed publication, its value comes from Dharwadker himself, a Machine Learning Engineering Manager at Meta with nearly a decade of experience and a Master's degree from Columbia University. However, his position at Meta may bias his perspective toward presenting the company positively, and the conversational format may lack the depth of formal research.

Smith, A. (2018, September 5). Many Facebook users don't understand how the site's news feed works. Pew Research Center.
This source holds strong relevance to the subtopic of social media algorithms and echo chambers, as it provides concrete data on how little Facebook users understand the algorithm that controls what they see. It shows how echo chambers form without users even realizing it, as they continue to consume one-sided content without questioning it. In terms of credibility, this source comes from the Pew Research Center, one of the most respected and widely cited nonpartisan research centers in the world, and the survey is based on a nationally representative sample of Facebook users. However, a limitation is that it was published in 2018, meaning it may not fully reflect how users interact with today's more complex algorithms.

02 — Anonymity & Toxic Behavior Online +
Lapidot-Lefler, N., & Barak, A. (2012). Effects of anonymity, invisibility, and lack of eye contact on toxic online disinhibition. Computers in Human Behavior, 28(2), 434-443.
This article is relevant to the subtopic of anonymity and toxic behavior online, as it directly identifies how being unidentifiable and a lack of social cues remove restraint in online behavior, explaining why toxic behavior is more common online than in real life. In terms of credibility, this source is published in a peer-reviewed academic journal and uses an experimental method to isolate the specific conditions that encourage harmful behavior, minimizing outside interference. It also appears free of explicit bias as no ethnic, racial, or age distinctions were made, making it a reliable source for this topic.
FIND MORE ↗
Kim, H. (2024, March 11). 1 in 5 Korean teenagers experiences cyberbullying: survey. The Korea Times.
This source is relevant to the subtopic of anonymity and toxic behavior online as it provides real-world evidence of how toxic interactions occur among Korean teenagers, showing that cyberbullying is not theoretical but a widespread experience that affects a significant portion of teens in their daily lives. In terms of credibility, this source is published in the Korea Times, a reputable English newspaper in South Korea, and is based on survey data collected from over 100 students, making its findings broadly applicable. However, because it is a news article rather than a peer-reviewed publication, it may lack the depth and rigor of formal academic research.

03 — Cancel Culture +
Clark, M. D. (2024). From calling out to cancel culture. In M. D. Clark, We tried to tell y'all: Black Twitter and the rise of digital counternarratives (Oxford Studies in Digital Politics). Oxford University Press.
This source is relevant to the subtopic of cancel culture, as it provides the cultural and historical background of how these practices developed from earlier forms of activism and boycotts used by marginalized communities, showing that cancel culture is not just a recent internet trend but part of a longer tradition of holding powerful people accountable. It also explains how social media has changed these forms of protest by allowing information to spread quickly and reach larger audiences. In terms of credibility, Meredith D. Clark is a widely recognized media scholar and professor whose research focuses on digital media, race, and online communication. Her work is grounded in academic media studies and historical analysis, making it a credible source for understanding digital culture and social media activism.
FIND MORE ↗
Ng, E. (2020). No grand pronouncements here: Reflections on cancel culture and digital media participation. Television & New Media, 21(6), 621-627.
This source is relevant to the subtopic of cancel culture as it examines how social media platforms create an environment where cancel culture can occur. It also raises the important question of whether cancel culture is effective in holding people accountable or simply a way of unfair judgment, connecting directly to the broader topic of internet controversy. In terms of credibility, this article is a peer-reviewed academic journal by SAGE, and is written by Eve Ng, an associate professor at Ohio University, whose research specifically addresses the cultural and political significance of digital media, making this a reliable and academically grounded source.

04 — Spread of Misinformation +
Vosoughi, S., Roy, D., & Aral, S. (2018). The spread of true and false news online. Science, 359(6380), 1146-1151.
This source directly supports the argument that misinformation spreads rapidly online, reinforcing why false claims made during cancel culture events are so hard to correct. The study found that false news spreads faster, farther, and more broadly than true news on Twitter, largely because false stories tend to be more novel and emotionally provocative. In terms of credibility, this is a landmark peer-reviewed study published in Science, one of the most prestigious academic journals in the world, conducted by researchers at MIT using a dataset of 126,000 stories shared by 3 million users over 11 years, making it highly rigorous and reliable.

Social media is a huge part of everyday life, especially for high school students. It is where people get news, form opinions, and interact with others, which means it has a strong influence on how people think and act. Problems like algorithm-driven echo chambers, online anonymity and toxicity, and cancel culture may seem like separate issues, but they are closely connected. Together, they create an online environment where people see limited perspectives, act without accountability, and react too quickly. While social media can be useful, these combined effects make online spaces more harmful than they first appear.
